A Journey Through the History of Corn

Indigenous people: Corn was an essential food for the Aztec, Mayan and Inca civilizations, serving as the basis of the diet and playing a crucial role in their cultures and rituals.
Introduction to Europe: In the 16th century, Spanish explorers brought corn to Europe, where it quickly became a popular food and adapted to different climates and soil conditions.
Global food: Today, corn is one of the most cultivated and consumed cereals in the world, with a fundamental role in global food security.

Growing Corn: From Field to Table

Diverse varieties: Corn comes in a wide variety of types, from classic yellow to colorful varieties such as red, blue and black, each with distinct characteristics and uses.
Climate and soil: Corn adapts to different climates, from tropical regions to temperate climates, and prefers fertile, well-drained soils.
Planting and harvesting: Corn planting generally occurs in spring or early summer, and harvesting takes place about three months later, when the kernels mature and reach their characteristic golden color.
Growing techniques: Corn growing techniques vary depending on the region and type of corn, but may include irrigation, fertilization, pest and disease control, and mechanical or manual harvesting.
